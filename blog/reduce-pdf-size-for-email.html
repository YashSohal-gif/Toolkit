Why won't my PDF fit in an email?

Most inboxes cap attachments at 20–25MB. Here's what actually makes a PDF that large, and how to shrink it without turning your document into mush.

What actually makes a PDF huge

It's almost never the text. A page of pure typed text — even a 200-page contract — is usually a few hundred KB total, because text in a PDF is just a small set of instructions ("draw this word at this position"), not a picture of the word. The size problem is nearly always images: a scanned page, a screenshot pasted in, or a photo dropped into a Word document before it was exported to PDF. A single photo straight off a phone camera can be 3–8MB — multiply that by 10 scanned pages and you have a 50MB "document."

Scanned documents are the worst offender

When you scan a paper page, the scanner captures it as a high-resolution photograph of the whole page — usually at 300 DPI or higher, in full color, even if the page is just black text on white. That's enormously wasteful: a black-and-white page doesn't need 16 million colors per pixel, and it rarely needs to be sharp enough to zoom into individual letters. This is exactly why compressors that re-render each page as a smaller, lower-color-depth image can shrink a scanned PDF by 80–95% with no visible loss in an ordinary read.

Why "just zip it" doesn't help much

Zipping a PDF barely shrinks it, because the images and fonts inside a PDF are usually already compressed (JPEG images, for instance, are already about as compressed as they'll get). Zip compression works by finding repeated patterns in a file — but a well-compressed image has very little repetition left to squeeze out. The size reduction has to happen inside the PDF, by re-encoding the images at a smaller size or lower quality, not by wrapping the whole file in another compression layer.

What actually works: target a size, not a "quality" slider

Most compression tools ask you to pick a vague "quality" level and hope for the best. A better approach is compressing toward an exact target size — e.g. "get this under 20MB for Gmail" or "under 10MB for this college portal" — and letting the tool iteratively reduce quality and resolution only as much as needed to hit that number, so you're never giving up more clarity than necessary.

Does compressing a PDF remove selectable text?

It depends on the method. A tool that rasterizes each page into a smaller image (the approach that gets the biggest size reductions on scanned documents) does turn any real text into part of the image, meaning it's no longer selectable or searchable afterward. If your PDF is a genuine digital document — actual selectable text, not a scan — check whether it needs compressing at all, since text-based PDFs are rarely the ones hitting email attachment limits in the first place.
